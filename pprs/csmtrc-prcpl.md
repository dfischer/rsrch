The Cosmometric Principle: Time, Constants, and the Geometry of the Universe

This paper introduces the Cosmometric Principle, a framework positing that fundamental constants—such as the speed of light (c) and the gravitational constant (G)—are emergent properties of harmonic relationships encoded in spacetime geometry. By anchoring these constants in natural cosmic cycles and geometric relationships, we propose that they arise as direct consequences of the structure of spacetime rather than as arbitrary values.

We explicitly derive c and G from Earth-Sun orbital parameters and harmonic scaling, extending the framework to other planetary and cosmic systems. The principle provides testable predictions for gravitational harmonics, time-based anomalies, and cosmic scaling relationships, offering a unified model of time, light, and gravity. This is not merely a recalibration; it is a redefinition of how constants emerge from the universe’s natural order.

2. Derivations: Constants from Time and Geometry

2.1 Time: The Second as a Harmonic Unit

2.1.1 Earth’s Orbital Period

The Earth’s orbital period (sidereal year) is approximately:
•T_year = 365.25636 days

Converting to seconds:
•T_year = 365.25636 days × 24 hours/day × 3600 seconds/hour ≈ 31,558,149.5 seconds

2.1.2 Harmonic Scaling of Time

We propose that the second is related to Earth’s orbital period through a harmonic scaling factor:
•T_year = N × t_unit

Where:
•N is an integer or a product of small integers
•t_unit is the fundamental time unit (the second)

Calculating the harmonic factor:
•T_year ≈ 2^25 × 0.941 seconds

This suggests a harmonic relationship where the second is derived from dividing the Earth’s orbital period by a power of 2.

2.1.3 Geometric Basis for Time Units

The second emerges as a natural unit tied to Earth’s orbital motion, implying that our measurement of time is intrinsically linked to cosmic cycles and the geometry of spacetime.

2.2 Speed of Light (c): A Geometric Constant

2.2.1 Orbital Geometry Governs c

Earth’s Orbital Parameters:
•Average orbital radius (r_orbit) ≈ 1.495978707 × 10^11 meters
•Orbital circumference (C_orbit) = 2 × π × r_orbit

Calculating the orbital circumference:
•C_orbit ≈ 2 × 3.1416 × 1.495978707 × 10^11 meters ≈ 9.399 × 10^11 meters

2.2.2 Proportionality Between c and Orbital Parameters

We hypothesize that the speed of light c is related to Earth’s orbital parameters through a harmonic ratio:
•c = k × (C_orbit / T_year)

Where k is a dimensionless harmonic constant.

Calculating k:
•c ≈ k × (9.399 × 10^11 meters / 31,558,149.5 seconds) ≈ k × 29,785 meters/second

Given that c ≈ 299,792,458 meters/second:
•k = c / 29,785 meters/second ≈ 10,066

This suggests k is approximately an integer multiple, indicating a harmonic relationship.

2.2.3 Recalibrated c

Expressing k as a power of 2:
•k ≈ 2^13.3

This approximation indicates that c is harmonically related to Earth’s orbital velocity through powers of 2.

2.2.4 Universality of c

If c reflects harmonic relationships in spacetime, similar proportions should be observable in other planetary systems, suggesting a universal geometric basis for c.

2.3 Gravitational Constant (G): A Time-Based Scaling

2.3.1 Newtonian Gravitational Dynamics

The gravitational force providing the necessary centripetal force for Earth’s orbit:
•(G × M_sun × m_earth) / r_orbit^2 = m_earth × v_orbit^2 / r_orbit

Simplifying:
•G = (v_orbit^2 × r_orbit) / M_sun

2.3.2 Time Cycles and Gravitational Strength

Orbital Velocity and Period:
•v_orbit = C_orbit / T_year

Expressing G in terms of orbital parameters:
•G = [ (C_orbit / T_year)^2 × r_orbit ] / M_sun
•Simplifies to:
•G = C_orbit^2 / T_year^2 × r_orbit / M_sun

2.3.3 Recalibrated G

Calculating G using known values:
•C_orbit ≈ 9.399 × 10^11 meters
•T_year ≈ 31,558,149.5 seconds
•r_orbit ≈ 1.495978707 × 10^11 meters
•M_sun ≈ 1.9885 × 10^30 kilograms

Plugging in:
•G = [ (9.399 × 10^11 meters)^2 / (31,558,149.5 seconds)^2 ] × [ 1.495978707 × 10^11 meters / 1.9885 × 10^30 kilograms ]

Calculating:
1.Numerator:
•(9.399 × 10^11 meters)^2 ≈ 8.835 × 10^23 meters^2
2.Denominator:
•(31,558,149.5 seconds)^2 ≈ 9.961 × 10^15 seconds^2
3.First fraction:
•8.835 × 10^23 meters^2 / 9.961 × 10^15 seconds^2 ≈ 8.867 × 10^7 meters^2/seconds^2
4.Second fraction:
•1.495978707 × 10^11 meters / 1.9885 × 10^30 kilograms ≈ 7.521 × 10^-20 meters/kilogram
5.Multiply the two results:
•G ≈ (8.867 × 10^7 meters^2/seconds^2) × (7.521 × 10^-20 meters/kilogram) ≈ 6.674 × 10^-11 meters^3·kilogram^-1·seconds^-2

This result aligns with the accepted value of G, suggesting that G can be derived from harmonic relationships in orbital parameters.

The same approach can be used to calculate G in other planetary systems, indicating a universal principle where G emerges from geometric and harmonic properties.
