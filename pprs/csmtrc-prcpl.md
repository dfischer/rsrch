The Cosmometric Principle: Time, Constants, and the Geometry of the Universe

This paper introduces the Cosmometric Principle, a framework positing that fundamental constants—such as the speed of light (c) and the gravitational constant (G)—are emergent properties of harmonic relationships encoded in spacetime geometry. By anchoring these constants in natural cosmic cycles and geometric relationships, we propose that they arise as direct consequences of the structure of spacetime rather than as arbitrary values.

We explicitly derive c and G from Earth-Sun orbital parameters and harmonic scaling, extending the framework to other planetary and cosmic systems. The principle provides testable predictions for gravitational harmonics, time-based anomalies, and cosmic scaling relationships, offering a unified model of time, light, and gravity. This is not merely a recalibration; it is a redefinition of how constants emerge from the universe’s natural order.

1. Introduction: The Cosmometric Challenge

1.1 The Fragmentation of Physics

Modern physics relies on fundamental constants such as c and G, yet their origins remain unexplained:
1.The Speed of Light (c): Considered a universal constant, c has a specific value with no underlying rationale provided by current theories.
2.The Gravitational Constant (G): Treated as an empirically measured parameter, G appears disconnected from other fundamental quantities like time cycles or geometry.
3.Time Units: The second is an anthropocentric construct, defined by atomic transitions, with no apparent connection to natural cosmic cycles.

This fragmentation obscures the simplicity of nature’s underlying structure, resulting in disconnected measurements and constants.

1.2 The Cosmometric Principle

The Cosmometric Principle asserts:

Fundamental constants and measurements are not independent but emerge naturally from harmonic relationships encoded in spacetime geometry.

This principle aims to:
1.Tie Fundamental Constants to Natural Time Cycles: Link units like the second to cosmic periods such as Earth’s orbital period.
2.Derive Constants Directly from Geometry and Dynamics: Show that c and G can be derived from geometric relationships and orbital parameters.
3.Extend Universally: Apply these relationships to other planetary and cosmic systems, unifying time, light, and gravity.

2. Derivations: Constants from Time and Geometry

2.1 Time: The Second as a Harmonic Unit

2.1.1 Earth’s Orbital Period

The Earth’s orbital period (sidereal year) is approximately:
•T_year = 365.25636 days

Converting to seconds:
•T_year = 365.25636 days × 24 hours/day × 3600 seconds/hour ≈ 31,558,149.5 seconds

2.1.2 Harmonic Scaling of Time

We propose that the second is related to Earth’s orbital period through a harmonic scaling factor:
•T_year = N × t_unit

Where:
•N is an integer or a product of small integers
•t_unit is the fundamental time unit (the second)

Calculating the harmonic factor:
•T_year ≈ 2^25 × 0.941 seconds

This suggests a harmonic relationship where the second is derived from dividing the Earth’s orbital period by a power of 2.

2.1.3 Geometric Basis for Time Units

The second emerges as a natural unit tied to Earth’s orbital motion, implying that our measurement of time is intrinsically linked to cosmic cycles and the geometry of spacetime.

2.2 Speed of Light (c): A Geometric Constant

2.2.1 Orbital Geometry Governs c

Earth’s Orbital Parameters:
•Average orbital radius (r_orbit) ≈ 1.495978707 × 10^11 meters
•Orbital circumference (C_orbit) = 2 × π × r_orbit

Calculating the orbital circumference:
•C_orbit ≈ 2 × 3.1416 × 1.495978707 × 10^11 meters ≈ 9.399 × 10^11 meters
